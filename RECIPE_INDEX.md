# Shabbat Slow-Cooked Stews

*The crown jewels of Shabbat cooking — dishes buried in embers or kept warm overnight*

---

## 1. Adafina

**ID:** `adafina`

Adafina, meaning "buried," recalls the historical method of burying pots in embers to slow-cook overnight. This Moroccan Shabbat morning stew traditionally features layers of chickpeas, potatoes, and wheat. This plant-based adaptation replaces meat with savory seitan, while maintaining the dish's signature caramelized depth and comforting richness.

---

## 2. Wheat Berries (Hita / Trigo)

**ID:** `adafinawheatside_dish`

Hita (חיטה) is Hebrew for wheat, and Trigo is the Spanish word meaning the same. In Tunisian Arabic, wheat is also called Hita. These whole wheat berries are toasted with aromatics and enclosed in a cloth bag to cook overnight in the Shabbat stew, absorbing its rich flavors and becoming a tender, savory pudding.

---

## 3. Tfina (Seitan and Barley Stew)

**ID:** `tfina_stew`

Tfina comes from the Arabic root meaning "to bury," referring to pots buried in embers for Shabbat. This vegan adaptation replaces beef with hearty seitan while maintaining the Djerban tradition of caramelizing sugar. The result is a dark, savory stew rich with potatoes and barley perfect for lunch.

---

## 4. Cholent (Hamin)

**ID:** `cholent`

Cholent, called Hamin in Hebrew, is the Ashkenazi slow-cooked Shabbat stew. It is the Eastern European equivalent to Moroccan Adafina and Tunisian Tfina. The name 'Cholent' derives from the Old French 'chalant' meaning 'warm'. All three dishes share the same purpose: food kept warm overnight for the Shabbat morning meal.
