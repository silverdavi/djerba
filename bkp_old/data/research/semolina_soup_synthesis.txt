## Semolina Soup (שמיד): A Cultural & Culinary Exploration with Vegan Adaptation

This report explores the traditional Tunisian Jewish Semolina Soup, known as "שמיד" (Shamid) in Hebrew, with a focus on its cultural significance and a practical strategy for creating a delicious and authentic vegan version.  This is a classic *erev* (evening meal) soup.

**1. Cultural Background & Etymology**

*   **Name:** The name "שמיד" is derived from "סולת" (Solet), the Hebrew word for semolina. Semolina itself refers to coarse wheat middlings, a staple in Mediterranean and North African cuisine.
*   **Djerban Jewish Heritage:**  This soup is deeply rooted in the culinary traditions of the Jewish community of Djerba, Tunisia, a community with a history spanning over two millennia. The dish reflects the blending of regional influences with the preservation of unique Jewish traditions.  It exemplifies *culinary syncretism*, sustaining cultural identity through food.
*   **Mizrahi and Sephardic Influences:** Semolina Soup is linked to broader Mizrahi Jewish cuisine, which encompasses the Middle Eastern and North African traditions. The use of semolina and spices like paprika reflects both local and Sephardic influences. Sephardic Jews, originating from Spain, influenced North African cuisines by introducing Mediterranean ingredients and techniques.

**2. Traditional Preparation Overview**

Traditional Semolina Soup is characterized by its simplicity and nourishing qualities. The process generally involves:

*   Lightly toasting semolina in oil or butter.
*   Sautéing onions for sweetness and flavor depth.
*   Simmering the semolina and onions in broth or water until it thickens into a smooth consistency.
*   Seasoning primarily with paprika.

The soup is relatively quick and easy to prepare, making it suitable for both daily meals and special occasions.  It is especially favored during cooler seasons and festive periods. It can be prepared in advance and kept warm which also made it suitable for Shabbat meals.

**3. Vegan Adaptation Strategy**

Since traditional Semolina Soup sometimes uses meat broth or ground beef, adapting it for vegans will require some adjustment. The key is to:

*   **Maintain the Core Flavors:** Focus on preserving the essential flavors of toasted semolina, caramelized onions, and paprika, which are inherently vegan.
*   **Replace Animal Products Thoughtfully:** Substitute animal-based ingredients with plant-based alternatives that provide similar texture and umami depth.
*   **Utilize Traditional Vegan-Friendly Elements:** Incorporate regional ingredients like preserved lemons, capers, and harissa, which are vegan and common in Tunisian cuisine.
*   **Embrace the Spirit of Adaptation:** Recognize that culinary traditions are dynamic and that adapting recipes to dietary needs is a natural part of cultural evolution.

**5. Cultural Significance & Family Context (Silver-Cohen-Trabelsi Family)**

*   **Dish Type:** Considered an *erev* (evening meal) soup.
*   **Family History:**  Within the Silver-Cohen-Trabelsi family, Semolina Soup (מרק סולת עם בצל ופפריקה) is a cherished dish, passed down through generations. Its simplicity and affordability made it a staple, particularly for evening meals.
*   **Cultural Role:**  The soup's adaptability and cultural context resonate with the diaspora. Its ingredients offer a way for those from that community to hold on to their family and cultural identity, no matter their location in the world.

**6. Preparation Tips & Techniques (Vegan Version)**

1.  **Toast the Semolina:** Toasting the semolina in olive oil deepens its flavor and prevents it from becoming gummy. Do this over medium heat, stirring constantly, until lightly golden.
2.  **Caramelize the Onions:** Sauté the onions slowly over low heat until they are deeply caramelized. This process develops sweetness and richness. Don't rush it!
3.  **Layer the Flavors:** Add paprika and any other spices to the onions during the last few minutes of sautéing to bloom their flavors.
4.  **Simmer Gently:** Add the vegetable broth and the cooked semolina-onion mixture to a pot. Bring to a simmer and cook gently until the semolina is cooked through and the soup has thickened to your desired consistency. This may take 15-20 minutes.
5.  **Umami Boost:** Consider adding a small amount of tomato paste, or mushroom broth to enhance the savory flavor. Dried or fresh mushrooms simmered with the broth also work.
6.  **Preserved Lemons & Capers (Optional):**  Finely chop preserved lemon rind and add it to the soup for brightness.  Capers add a salty, briny flavor. Use sparingly!
7.  **Adjust Seasoning:** Taste and adjust the seasoning with salt, pepper, and additional paprika as needed. Remember that preserved lemons and capers contribute salt, so be cautious.
8.  **Protein Addition:** If using TVP, rehydrate according to package directions and add it to the soup during the last 10 minutes of cooking. Lentils should be cooked separately until soft and added at the end. Sauté mushrooms before adding to the broth.
9.  **Consistency:** This is meant to be a comforting soup, not necessarily a thin broth. Adjust the amount of liquid depending on your personal taste.
10. **Garnish:** Serve the soup hot, garnished with a drizzle of olive oil and chopped fresh herbs like parsley or cilantro (optional).  A dollop of vegan harissa paste on the side adds a spicy kick.

By carefully substituting animal-based ingredients with plant-based alternatives and respecting the traditional flavors of Semolina Soup, a delicious and culturally authentic vegan version can be created, keeping the rich heritage of the Djerban Jewish community alive.
