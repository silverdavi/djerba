Semolina Soup (שמיד) in Tunisian Jewish cuisine from Djerba is a traditional dish with cultural, historical, and culinary significance. Here is a detailed exploration based on your points of interest:

## 1. Etymology and Meaning of the Name  
The name "שמיד" (pronounced "Shamid") in Hebrew is associated with "semolina" (סולת), referring to the primary ingredient, semolina flour. Semolina, a coarse wheat middling, is commonly used in Mediterranean and North African cooking. The word itself is rooted in the Mediterranean culinary lexicon where semolina-based soups and porridges are traditional staples.

## 2. Historical Significance in the Djerban Jewish Community  
In the Jewish community of Djerba, Tunisia, semolina soup carries significance as a simple, nourishing dish that reflects the blending of regional influences. The Jews of Djerba have a long history dating back over two millennia, preserving unique traditions while incorporating Mediterranean and Arabic flavors. Semolina soup, often flavored with onions and paprika as described by your family, exemplifies this culinary syncretism and sustains a cultural identity through food, especially during times meant for comfort and community such as the Sabbath and holidays.

## 3. Traditional Preparation Methods and Ingredients  
Traditionally, the semolina soup in Djerban Jewish cuisine is prepared by toasting or lightly frying semolina in oil or butter, then simmering it in a broth or water until it thickens into a smooth, warm soup. Key ingredients include:  
- Semolina (coarse wheat flour)  
- Onions, typically sautéed to bring out sweetness  
- Paprika, which adds a characteristic warm, smoky flavor  
- Olive oil or a similar fat for sautéing  
- Water or broth as the base liquid  

The dish is relatively simple yet flavorful, relying on the quality of the base ingredients and the aromatic power of the paprika and onion to provide a comforting experience.

## 4. Cultural Context (Sabbath, Holidays, Daily Meals)  
Semolina soup serves as a versatile dish in the Djerban Jewish context:  
- It can be part of daily meals as an economical and nourishing dish.  
- It is suitable for Shabbat and holiday meals because it can be easily prepared ahead of time and kept warm, fitting kosher practices around Sabbath cooking restrictions.  
- Its comforting texture and warm spices make it ideal during cooler seasons and festive occasions.

## 5. Regional Variations Across North African Jewish Communities  
Across North Africa, Jewish communities have semolina-based dishes that vary by local tastes and available ingredients:  
- In Tunisia and Libya, semolina soups or porridges often include a blend of Mediterranean spices such as cumin, turmeric, and paprika.  
- Moroccan Jews tend to prepare semolina in forms closer to couscous dishes or sweetened semolina puddings, differing from the savory and simpler Djerban style.  
- In some communities, semolina soups might be enriched with meat broths or vegetables, or served with bread, according to local culinary norms.

## 6. Differences Between Tunisian and Moroccan Jewish Culinary Traditions  
While both Tunisian and Moroccan Jewish cuisines share Mediterranean and Arab influences, notable differences include:  
- Tunisian Jewish cuisine, including Djerba, is characterized by simpler, spicier dishes often employing paprika and hot peppers, likely influenced by Sicilian and Mediterranean neighbors.  
- Moroccan Jewish dishes frequently emphasize slow-cooked stews like Dafina and incorporate sweeter spice blends, often with dried fruits and nuts.  
- Semolina in Moroccan Jewish cuisine is more often linked with couscous and baked dishes, whereas Tunisian Jewish cuisine uses it in straightforward soups or porridges.

## 7. Connection to Sephardic or Mizrahi Culinary Traditions  
The semolina soup in Djerban Jewish cuisine ties closely to broader **Mizrahi Jewish cuisine**, which encompasses Middle Eastern and North African Jewish culinary traditions. The use of semolina, aromatic spices like paprika, cumin, and turmeric, and cooking methods reflect both local and Sephardic influences.  

- Sephardic Jews, who originated from Spain and later settled in North Africa, influenced local cuisines by introducing Mediterranean ingredients and techniques.  
- Mizrahi cuisine, involving communities from across the Middle East and North Africa, shares the use of staple grains, legumes, and spices, with kosher dietary laws shaping the dishes.  

Thus, semolina soup (שמיד) can be seen as a part of this shared heritage, combining local Tunisian ingredients and Sephardic-Mizrahi culinary elements.
